Research

Exploring data management, predictive mental health, and tools for civic organizations

Research Focus

My research sits at the intersection of human-computer interaction, data management practices, and mental health technologies. I investigate how computing practitioners—from HCI researchers to civic and non-profit data workers—navigate the complexities of working with sensitive data, and develop systems and frameworks that support ethical practices centered on data flourishing: human-centered approaches that prioritize dignity and well-being over mere compliance.

As AI-driven mental health interventions become increasingly pervasive, the gap between researcher intentions and institutional support creates significant risks for vulnerable populations. Mental health technology research operates in a liminal space—neither HIPAA-regulated nor clearly governed by existing computing ethics—leaving practitioners to make high-stakes decisions without adequate guidance.

My research centers on operationalizing data practices that support human flourishing—moving from compliance-focused approaches toward care-centered, contextually-sensitive methods honoring individual dignity and collective responsibility.

Current Work

Mental Health Data Ethics and Practitioner Challenges

Research Team Member

Ongoing

Through semi-structured interviews with eleven HCI researchers developing predictive and semi-automated mental health systems, our research team investigated the disconnect between ethical intentions and practical capabilities in mental health technology research.

  • Conducted thematic analysis revealing systematic challenges in mental health tech development
  • Identified gaps between diverse disciplinary training leading to inconsistent ethical practices
  • Documented IRB struggles to evaluate emerging technologies like large language models
  • Revealed fundamental ambiguity around what constitutes "health data" in computing research
  • Exposed significant gaps between published methods and actual research practices
  • Demonstrated how field fragmentation leaves practitioners without adequate support for vulnerable populations

Datum Fieldnotes: Supporting Civic and Non-Profit Data Work

Lead Developer

Ongoing

In collaboration with Dr. Betsy DiSalvo and Dr. Annabel Rothschild, developed a Google Sheets add-on supporting data documentation in civic and non-profit (CNP) organizations. The tool addresses a critical tension: data work is mission-critical for demonstrating impact, yet diverts resources from direct service.

  • Conducted user testing with twelve CNP data workers to understand organizational needs
  • Developed tool that automates micro-documentation and enables rich contextualization
  • Created comprehensive curriculum to support data infrastructure literacy across skill levels
  • Addressed challenges of lean budgets, high turnover, and minimal training capacity
  • Recognized that CNP datasets frequently become training data for ML systems in social services
  • Published findings at CHI 2025 Late Breaking Work

Datum Fieldnotes: Easing the Data Work Burden on Civic and Non-Profit Organizations

CHI Late Breaking Work

Co-Author

This work presents Datum Fieldnotes, a Google Sheets add-on designed to streamline data documentation and note-taking processes for civic and non-profit organizations. Through user testing with twelve CNP data workers and qualitative analysis, we demonstrate how thoughtful design of data management tools can significantly reduce burden on organizations working with limited resources while maintaining data quality and integrity. The tool automates micro-documentation and enables rich contextualization, addressing the tension between mission-critical data work and resource constraints.

Future Directions

Developing Responsible Predictive Mental Health Systems

Future Research

Building and evaluating predictive mental health technologies leveraging machine learning analysis of digital behavioral traces—social media content, smartphone sensing data, and online community interactions—while addressing the ethical challenges identified in current research.

  • Investigating continuous consent models maintaining meaningful participant engagement
  • Developing semi-automated intervention systems grounded in therapeutic practice
  • Establishing evaluation frameworks assessing both predictive accuracy and actual impact on well-being
  • Exploring responsible integration of large language models into mental health support systems
  • Implementing safeguards beyond current institutional requirements
  • Developing transparency mechanisms that acknowledge system limitations and potential harms

Building Community-Driven Governance

Future Research

Developing adaptive, principles-based protocols for mental health data management that respond to emerging technologies while protecting vulnerable populations. Moving beyond rigid compliance frameworks toward governance mechanisms supporting sophisticated ethical reasoning.

  • Investigating context-aware approaches to "public but personal" data from mental health communities
  • Developing protocols for data technically accessible but intended for specific audiences
  • Creating governance mechanisms that make invisible ethical reasoning visible in published work
  • Supporting practitioners' existing sophisticated ethical reasoning with systematic frameworks

Bridging Disciplinary Boundaries

Future Research

Mental health technology development requires sustained collaboration between computing and clinical communities, yet these groups often publish in different venues, follow different norms, and maintain different data handling standards. This research aims to make such partnerships more feasible and sustainable.

  • Examining how data use agreements function in practice across disciplines
  • Investigating participatory consent models across disciplinary contexts
  • Balancing participant privacy with methodological transparency for rigorous science
  • Supporting researchers without established clinical networks in forming collaborations
